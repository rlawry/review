HS-ESS1-2 — Construct an explanation of the Big Bang theory based on astronomical evidence of light spectra, motion of distant galaxies, and composition of matter in the universe.

Base your answers to questions 1–3 on the passage below and on your knowledge of Earth Science.

Light from nearly all distant galaxies is shifted toward the red end of the spectrum (redshift), indicating galaxies are moving away from Earth. The farther a galaxy is, the greater its redshift. The universe is approximately 75% hydrogen and 25% helium by mass — proportions consistent with Big Bang nucleosynthesis models. Faint microwave radiation (cosmic microwave background) fills the universe uniformly, representing the afterglow of the early, hot universe. Together, these observations support the Big Bang theory: the universe began ~13.8 billion years ago as an extremely hot, dense point and has been expanding ever since.

HS-ESS1-3 — Communicate scientific ideas about the way stars, over their life cycle, produce elements.

Base your answers to questions 1–3 on the passage and table below and on your knowledge of Earth Science.

All elements heavier than hydrogen and helium were forged inside stars through stellar nucleosynthesis. Main-sequence stars fuse hydrogen into helium. More massive stars in later stages can fuse helium into carbon, oxygen, silicon, and up to iron (Fe, atomic number 26). Iron cannot release energy through fusion, so massive stars explode as supernovae, producing and dispersing elements heavier than iron (including gold and uranium) throughout space. These elements eventually become part of new stars, planets, and living organisms.

HS-ESS1-5 — Evaluate evidence of the past and current movements of continental and oceanic crust and the theory of plate tectonics to explain the ages of crustal rocks.

Base your answers to questions 1–3 on the passage below and on your knowledge of Earth Science.

At mid-ocean ridges, new oceanic crust forms continuously as magma rises and solidifies (seafloor spreading). Radiometric dating confirms that oceanic crust near ridges is younger than crust farther away. The oldest oceanic crust is ~200 million years old; continental crust can exceed 4 billion years. Symmetrical magnetic striping patterns on the ocean floor — parallel to mid-ocean ridges — record periodic reversals of Earth's magnetic field and provided crucial evidence for seafloor spreading. At subduction zones, old oceanic crust is recycled back into the mantle, which is why oceanic crust is always much younger than continental crust.

HS-ESS1-6 — Apply scientific reasoning and evidence from ancient Earth materials, meteorites, and other planetary surfaces to construct an account of Earth and the solar system's formation and early history.

Base your answers to questions 1–3 on the passage below and on your knowledge of Earth Science.

Earth and the solar system formed ~4.6 billion years ago from a rotating cloud of gas and dust (the solar nebula). Evidence for this age comes from radiometric dating of ancient zircon crystals (~4.4 billion years old), meteorites (~4.56 billion years old), and lunar rocks (~4.5 billion years old). The similar ages and compositions of these materials support the solar nebula hypothesis. Early Earth was extremely hot due to meteorite bombardment and radioactive decay. Denser materials (iron, nickel) sank to form the core while less-dense silicates rose to form the crust — a process called differentiation.

Earth's surface features form through processes that operate across vastly different temporal scales. The Himalayas formed over ~50 million years as the Indian and Eurasian plates collided, rising a few cm/year. In contrast, a volcanic island can emerge within decades. Ocean trenches, the deepest features on Earth, form at subduction zones. The Mid-Atlantic Ridge spreads at ~2.5 cm/year, continuously generating new oceanic crust. At the surface, weathering and erosion remove thousands of meters of rock over millions of years, continuously reshaping landforms.

HS-ESS2-2 — Analyze geoscience data to make the claim that one change to Earth's surface can create feedbacks that cause changes to other Earth systems.

Base your answers to questions 1–3 on the passage below and on your knowledge of Earth Science.

In the Arctic, rising temperatures have caused significant melting of sea ice. Sea ice is white and reflects much of incoming solar radiation back into space (high albedo). When sea ice melts, it is replaced by darker ocean water, which absorbs more solar radiation (lower albedo). The absorbed energy further warms the ocean, causing more ice to melt — a self-reinforcing cycle called the ice-albedo feedback. Arctic sea ice extent has decreased by ~13% per decade since the late 1970s, with dramatic summer losses. This Arctic warming affects weather patterns across the Northern Hemisphere.

HS-ESS2-3 — Develop a model based on evidence of Earth's interior to describe the cycling of matter by thermally driven convection in the mantle.

Base your answers to questions 1–3 on the passage below and on your knowledge of Earth Science.

Earth's mantle (~84% of Earth's volume) consists of solid rock that behaves plastically over long temporal scales. Heat from Earth's core and radioactive decay within the mantle drives convection: hot, less-dense material rises toward the surface while cooler, denser material sinks. This convective cycling exerts drag forces on tectonic plates, driving seafloor spreading and subduction. Seismic wave analysis reveals regions of hot upwelling and cold downwelling material within the mantle. Despite convecting at only a few cm/year, mantle convection has reshaped Earth's entire surface over geologic time.

Water has unique properties that make it essential to Earth's surface processes. Its high specific heat capacity requires large amounts of energy to change temperature, moderating coastal climates. Unlike most substances, water expands when it freezes — causing mechanical weathering when water in cracks freezes and shatters rock (frost wedging). HS-ESS2-7 — Construct an argument based on evidence about the simultaneous coevolution of Earth's systems and life on Earth.
